Recognize the north-south trap

  • This lesson covers the moment your partner passes toward your head and surprises you in north-south.

Your partner deliberately moves toward your head to surprise you in this north-side position, so recognizing the trap early is what lets you start the counter in time rather than reacting after he has already settled there.

Push the elbow, swing the left leg across, and spin out

  • Push his elbow and shoulder so he cannot settle the position.
  • If he will not release, swing your left leg around his waist and across his body; when he drops his weight you are safe.
  • Place your left hand on his thigh and spin to face your partner - the spin twists his grip so he has to let go.

Pushing his elbow and shoulder stops him from settling his weight on top of you, and swinging your leg across his waist gives you the room to turn out from underneath him. Once your hand posts on his thigh and you spin to face him, holding the grip starts to twist his own wrist, which is exactly why he has to let go rather than keep fighting for it.

Counter the deep pass on the other side and spin up to guard

  • When he bench-presses his weight down onto you, slide your left leg underneath and cross it to the other side.
  • As he drops his weight, start the spin.
  • Finish in closed guard if his hands drop to the mat, or put your feet on his hips if he stays on his feet.

When he bench-presses his weight down onto your head from the other side, sliding your leg underneath and crossing it to that side lets you start the same spin from the new angle. If his hands drop to the mat as he commits his weight you can finish in closed guard; if he stays on his feet instead, replace your guard by posting your feet on his hips.

Drill the spin: leg across, push the shoulder

  • Get the other leg across and spin around in one motion.
  • Keep pushing his shoulder while the leg travels.

Drilling the movement without a pause is what makes it automatic under pressure: get the other leg across and spin around in one continuous motion, while you keep pushing his shoulder the whole time so he cannot resettle his weight before you finish turning to face him.

Answer the direction switch: right leg across, post on his knees

  • If he surprises you from the other side, your right leg goes across instead.
  • Post your right hand on his knees, then replace guard.
  • Repeat until the movement feels natural.

When he surprises you from the other direction, the same idea applies mirrored: your right leg goes across instead of your left, and posting your right hand on his knees gives you the frame to replace your guard on that side too. Repeating it from both directions is what makes the reaction automatic instead of something you have to think through.

Why the spin breaks his grip

  • Even when he stubbornly holds the pant grip, the spin twists his wrist and forces the release.
  • That grip break is the core of retaining guard from north-south.

Even when he stays stubborn and keeps holding onto the trouser grip, the spin itself is what twists his wrist until it hurts too much to hold on, forcing him to let it go. That twisting action - not brute strength - is the whole magic behind retaining guard from north-side position.
